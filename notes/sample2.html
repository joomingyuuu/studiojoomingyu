Review: Tino Sehgal at Leeum Museum of Art

JULY 10, 2026

“At some point, my interest in experimental art began to fade. [...] I used to be drawn to the peculiar exclusivity and self-referentiality of art—the way it connects only with its own history or with itself. Subjectively speaking, to me, it felt like a side path, a sanctuary from the norms of normalcy demanded by contemporary society. Its strong symbolic allure, and the self that could be constructed through taste, compensated for my lack of normalcy.”

“On the precipice of turning thirty, as I gradually needed complete economic independence, had to look after those around me, and began to ponder ways of living together, including marriage, my interest in experimental art drifted away as if saying, ‘That’s enough.’ While I felt a pang of regret for this distancing, on the other hand, it felt only natural. It didn't seem to be uniquely my experience either. [...] The vast majority of the audience looked like university students in their early to mid-twenties. After all, university is a time and space exempt from the general demands of society. / The audience is segregated, and so is the interest.”

— Lee Yeo-ro, Conversations with Filmmakers, Mediabus, p.277

A few years ago, back when I used to frequent art museums, I often wondered, "Why is it that the vast majority of people in museums are young?" Whenever the time accumulating in my body yielded answers to my past questions, instead of feeling like I was maturing, I couldn't shake the feeling that I was becoming a conventional, old-fashioned person. Driven by this, I would often commit reckless thoughts and actions under the vague premise of 'needing to find myself.' It was for this very reason that I went to the cinema for the first time in a long while.

Matinee movies have two advantages. One, of course, is that they are cheaper, and the other is that the audience is smaller than at other times. Much like in an art museum, an excessive number of viewers always ruins the pure appreciation of a work. Late-night movies can be preferred for the same reason. If time permits, museum exhibitions should also be visited on weekday afternoons as much as possible.

To return to the main point, I went to see Project Hail Mary, starring Ryan Gosling. Though I haven't read it, I understand it is based on an original novel. While watching, I felt the atmosphere was somewhat similar to The Martian, and as it turned out, both films were adapted from books by the same author. He seems to be a writer who excels at crafting stories with concepts highly attractive for film adaptation.

The protagonists of the film are 'Grace,' an Earthling, and 'Rocky,' an alien. Since Rocky is bound to be CGI, Ryan Gosling’s solo performance effectively commands nearly 70% of the film. In this kind of musical-like structure, the lead actor's capability inevitably becomes the single greatest factor influencing the quality of the work. Fortunately, Ryan Gosling’s performance was exceptional. I never found myself thinking, 'He is acting right now'; instead, I was naturally immersed in the narrative.

Leaving the theater, I had a bit of time left, so I stopped by two art museums. The Arko Art Center was hosting an exhibition titled Toad, Toad, Return of the Korean Pavilion: Home Time, reflecting on the Korean Pavilion at the Venice Biennale. Meanwhile, the Seoul National University Museum of Art (SNUMoA) was running The Flâneur in Errors, an exploration of error and identity in the digital age. Both museums offer free admission yet maintain a remarkably high caliber of exhibitions, making them my favorite places to visit whenever I have free time.

Both the film and the exhibitions certainly possessed their own depth, and there was absolutely no fault in taking the time to enjoy them. However, a thought kept lingering in my mind: contemporary art has still not escaped the discourse of postmodernism, repeating a cycle of self-replication. Fragmented identity, the alienation of the body, the empowerment of techno-capital, the concealment of dystopia, the politics of architecture, the temporality of place, the digital era, and artificial intelligence... The fact that these identical discussions are repeated is, in a way, rational for two reasons. First, it means we are still trapped within this precarious situation; second, we have yet to find an answer to it.

The fundamental question of Project Hail Mary can be condensed to: "What is a human being?" (Warning: Contains film spoilers.) The film's protagonist, Grace, is a loner with neither a dog nor an immediate family. Marginalized by mainstream academia, he holds a Ph.D. in biology but scrapes by as a middle school teacher. One day, when a global scientific crisis arises, a government agency forcibly drafts him for a perilous mission. He is thus placed aboard a one-way spaceship—essentially a death sentence. While the characters in the movie call Grace a hero, to us, he is viewed strictly as an individual utterly alienated by society.

The film's other protagonist, the alien Rocky, is a being from another planet facing the exact same crisis as Earth, who boarded a spaceship under a similar context. Through the course of a long voyage, both Rocky and Grace become the sole survivors of their respective ships. In that desperate situation, as they drift through the vacuum of space, their paths miraculously cross. Though their language, appearance, and everything else are entirely different, they quickly bond after their initial encounter under the shared circumstance of a singular goal.

At the SNUMoA exhibition The Flâneur in Errors, numerous works by artist Lee Young-wook are introduced, featuring distorted images that look as though an error occurred mid-upload. The primary subjects in his works are dogs, chimpanzees, and humans. While this could simply be a random selection of internet elements, considering the exhibition’s theme regarding existence in the 21st-century digital age, these three symbols hold quite significant meaning.

A 'dog' is an animal—biologically non-human. Yet, at the same time, dogs are treated as social beings possessing certain rights within modern human society. In the role of a companion, the dog has already been accepted as a family member in many nations. The situation for a 'chimpanzee' is 180 degrees different. Historically, chimpanzees have never been considered members of the human social community. Yet, as we know, they are one of the animals closest to humans. They share roughly 98% of our DNA, are capable of sophisticated communication and problem-solving using tools, and remarkably, a chimpanzee’s short-term working memory even surpasses that of humans (they are far more adept than us at card-memorization games).

Ultimately, the anxiety we harbor regarding this identity stems from the fact that the right to be deemed a 'human' does not rest upon biological (absolute) factors. A related discussion can be found in author Kim Hyun-kyung's book Person, Place, Hospitality. Here, the author argues that the criteria by which an individual is accepted as a 'person' depends entirely on the response of that society (place), rather than being something acquired innately or genetically.

The title of the Arko Art Center exhibition, Toad, Toad, Return of the Korean Pavilion: Home Time, borrows the familiar Korean children's folk song story of the toad ("I'll give you an old house, give me a new house") as a symbol for the fluidity and organic nature of place. The four participating artists interpret the Venice Biennale's Korean Pavilion through the lenses of 'nature,' 'state,' 'geology,' and 'politics,' respectively. The Korean Pavilion is an artificial structure nestled within a forest (Giardini Gardens: nature), yet it is also a symbolic site representing national identity as 'Koreans.' It is a building constructed upon soil that complexly binds organic and inorganic matter (a totem toward the sky), and it is simultaneously a political space situated among the pavilions of various nations in the 21st century.

Let us summarize the discussion so far. Human identity is not fixed. Its fluidity is determined by 'place.' And place, too, possesses volatility. In other words, 'humanhood' is a state shifted by shifting factors—meaning it is a second-order chaos. (Our 'society' can serve as another example of second-order chaos; society is shifted anew by the very theories that attempt to interpret it.) As is widely known, there is no clear way to decipher second-order chaos. Yes, it is a predictable conclusion, but we can never truly know who we are.

What, then, should we do? Let us retrace our discussion. First, we can talk about the various contexts of our place. We can explore the impact that a place has on me, and that I have on a place. Expanding further, we can discuss the interaction between ourselves and 'the world.' It is a classic principle: content dictates form, and form dictates content. And all of this discourse code-resolves back to that single film from the beginning, Project Hail Mary.

Grace and Rocky were beings from entirely separate worlds, isolated from one another, sharing none of the same environmental conditions. Yet, by the end of the film, they become irreplaceable friends and spend the rest of their lives together. If thinking that we can never truly know what the world is, or who I am living within it, feels daunting—could we not look at it differently? Does it not also mean that we can never truly be 'absolute strangers' to anyone else?

In the end, I have wound up right back at the postmodern narrative I so bitterly critiqued. I have gained nothing but a pun-like conclusion: the fact that “we know nothing” is identical to saying “we can become anything.” But even so, what choice do we have but to live our lives as earnestly as we can?
