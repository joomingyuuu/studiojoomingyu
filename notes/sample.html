Review: Tino Sehgal at Leeum Museum of Art

JULY 8, 2026

Joseph Campbell argues that the protagonists across various myths share the same narrative pattern. They meet a mentor to begin their adventure, make friends along the journey, and endure trials, but ultimately find love or a similarly happy ending. We can apply these three categories to the countless people who appear in our own lives: mentors, friends, and lovers.

Human relationships are divided into consanguineous (blood) and non-consanguineous relationships. The former consists of three types—parents, siblings, and spouses—each corresponding to the latter’s mentors, friends, and lovers. The positive emotions we can feel toward others can be categorized in the exact same way: the feeling toward a good mentor, a good friend, and a good lover. These can be defined as respect, friendship, and affection, respectively.

The Leeum Museum of Art is hosting a Tino Sehgal exhibition from March 3rd to June 28th, 2026. If you plan on going, I highly recommend visiting without any prior information. The core of Tino Sehgal’s exhibition lies within "the way the audience thinks" itself. It is best to exclude all external factors from your contemplation. From this point onward, I will be discussing the exhibition.

There are no artwork captions in this exhibition. Furthermore, there is no distinction between the inside and outside of the exhibition space. Leaving out captions is likely the artist's intentional message, urging us not to separate what is art from what is not. In the same vein, the artist continuously tells us not to distinguish between the inside and outside of the gallery. If there is no division between the artwork and the audience, nor between the inside and outside of the space, then what on earth is an exhibition? In the first place, what is art?

If I were to define art in a single phrase, it would be "the most persuasive form of attempt to express the indistinct." The rigidity of language is the most powerful tool that allows us to express various abstract concepts in the world, yet at the same time, it is our greatest obstacle. Within us lies an inherent desire to express that which cannot be put into words. Many 20th-century philosophers found that answer in art. Like all contemporary art, Tino Sehgal's exhibition is an attempt to "express the inexpressible," a lineage that flows directly from the 20th century.

What he wishes to convey through the exhibition is quite clear: the meaning of art lies in nothing other than "interaction." In other words, "experience." We are all observers and, at the same time, the subjects of observation. At the moment when the boundary between the observer and the observed collapses, what remains for us is ultimately "perspective." The essence of photography lies in the fact that, for the first time, humans became able to express not "the thing being seen," but "the way of seeing" itself. A photograph of a mother’s hand does not intend to tell us about the physical appearance of the hand; it tells us a story about "the perspective of looking at a mother's hand."

Cinema is an extension of photography. If photography symbolizes a gaze toward the world, cinema speaks of a perspective on life. And art (or fine art) is cinema in its most expanded form.

Tino Sehgal's current exhibition includes various contemporary art sculptures. Works by world-renowned artists—such as Balthasar Lovay, Claes Oldenburg, and Sol LeWitt, whom I particularly admire—are displayed without any explanation. Perhaps conscious of the exhibition being held in Korea, works by Suki Seokyeong Kang and the Korean-American artist John Pai were also installed, which was quite intriguing.

In the main space on the first floor, three jade-colored beaded curtains are arranged around the center, and the small areas divided by each curtain segment the interior of the gallery. This appears to be an attempt to create a sort of "sub-exhibition space" within the gallery. This small space, partitioned by the beaded curtains, is both an object of display in itself and a symbol of the space outside the exhibition. Stepping inside these curtains, the audience experiences watching the visitors and the artworks within the gallery—all of it—re-contextualized as a single piece of art. This recursive structure, flowing from the inside of the gallery to the artwork and out to the exterior, strongly reflects the artist’s argument to dismantle everything except for a single theme: "experience."

A good mentor makes my future more thrilling. A good friend allows me to remember my past more beautifully. And a good lover makes me forget both the past and the future entirely.

The nature-nurture debate can be more easily resolved by redefining the exact moment of human birth. When is a human born? When separated from the mother's body? When taking the first breath? When falling down for the first time? When falling in love for the first time? When experiencing the death of a loved one for the first time? Or is it when we realize that our existence is merely suspended precariously over the fluttering of relationships, memories, wounds, and coincidences?

If it is permitted to describe the process of growing old and dying as the world's longest suicide, then perhaps we are currently undergoing the world's longest experience of birth.
